## Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) often hallucinate in long-form generation. Existing approaches mainly improve factuality through post-hoc revision or reinforcement learning (RL) with correctness-based rewards, but these methods do not teach models to produce calibrated confidence estimates. As a result, models may still state incorrect claims confidently in their re-sponses. Recent advances in reasoning have significantly improved LLM performance, and have been leveraged to estimate confidence by incorpo-rating calibration into RL objectives. However, existing approaches remain limited to a single scalar confidence for the entire response, which is insuf-ficient for long-form generation where uncertainty varies across individual claims. To mitigate this problem, we propose CURE , a framework that improves long-form factuality by teaching LLMs to reason about uncer-tainty at the claim level. We first introduce a claim-aware reasoning protocol ,which structures outputs into atomic claims paired with explicit confidence estimates. We then develop a multi-stage training pipeline that aligns model confidence with claims’ correctness and then optimizes on factuality. The re-sulting calibrated confidence further enables selective prediction, allowing the model to abstain from uncertain claims at inference time. Experiments on four long-form factuality benchmarks show that CURE consistently improves factual accuracy over competitive supervised and RL baselines, while maintaining factual recall. In particular, it improves claim-level fac-tual accuracy by up to 39.9% on Biography generation. These gains are accompanied by improved calibration, as reflected by a 16.0% increase in AUROC on FactBench. 1

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities in generating long-form responses to open-ended queries (OpenAI, 2024; DeepSeek-AI, 2025; Team, 2025). However, they still suffer from factual errors, especially in long-form generation, where mistakes can compound across multiple claims and become difficult for users to verify (Zhang et al., 2025). A fundamental challenge is that models lack awareness of their own uncertainty: they state incorrect claims with high confidence, which undermines the factual reliability of their outputs. Accurate uncertainty estimation, or calibration, is therefore critical, as it enables models to distinguish reliable from unreliable claims, improving factuality while providing transparency to the user. Existing factuality improvement methods have mainly relied on post-hoc revision, where the model response is verified and corrected after generation (Dhuliawala et al., 2024; Chern et al., 2023). While effective, these methods introduce additional inference cost and do not improve the model’s intrinsic uncertainty awareness. More recently, training-time

## 2 Related Work 

Long-form Factuality in LLMs Prior work on long-form factuality can be grouped into three paradigms. Post-hoc revision methods verify and refine model outputs after gen-eration, either by decomposing responses into verification questions and revising accord-ingly (Dhuliawala et al., 2024), or by detecting errors with external tools and performing targeted correction (Chern et al., 2023). A second line of work incorporates factuality signals into RL training, using either step-wise verification to guide token-level updates (Li & Ng, 2025) or composite rewards that jointly optimize factual precision, response detail, and relevance (Chen et al., 2025). A third line focuses on fine-grained preference alignment, where external evaluators provide sentence- or claim-level supervision to construct higher-quality preference data (Huang & Chen, 2024; Gu et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2025). Despite their effectiveness, these approaches optimize only for correctness without modeling uncertainty, making it difficult to distinguish reliable claims from speculative ones. 

LLM Calibration Another line of work studies how to estimate and calibrate model uncertainty. Existing approaches include prompting models to output scalar confidence scores (Tian et al., 2023), estimating uncertainty via consistency across multiple sampled outputs (Kuhn et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023), and deriving confidence directly from token probabilities (Liu et al., 2024). More recent work incorporates calibration into RL by aug-menting correctness rewards with proper scoring rules such as the Brier score (Damani et al., 2025). However, these methods are primarily designed for short-form settings with a single confidence per response, or operate at a coarse response level that cannot capture claim-level uncertainty in long-form generation. Our work bridges these two directions by enabling claim-level calibration for long-form generation. We train the model to express uncertainty at the atomic claim level through structured reasoning, and decouple calibration from factuality optimization to avoid the interference observed in joint training. 

## 3 CURE: Claim-level Uncertainty-aware Resoning for Factual Long-Form Generation 

3.1 Problem Formulation 

Given an information-seeking query x, the model generates a factual response y. Such long-form responses typically contain multiple factual statements, which we represent as a set of atomic claims y = {c1, c2, ..., cN }, where each claim ci is a semantically coherent and independently verifiable content unit (e.g., "X was born in Y" as illustrated in Figure 1). For each claim ci, the model produces a confidence estimate pi between 0 and 1, reflecting its internal belief in the correctness of that claim. Let zi ∈ { 0, 1 } denote the ground-truth cor-rectness label of ci evaluated against external knowledge. Our goal is to improve long-form factuality by generating claims that are both factually correct and comprehensively address the query. To achieve this, the model must distinguish correct claims from speculative ones, which requires calibrated claim-level confidence estimates. To this end, we introduce a claim-aware reasoning protocol (§3.2) that structures outputs into atomic claims with explicit confidence, and a multi-stage training pipeline (§3.3) that induces a feasible reasoning space, aligns confidence with correctness, and optimizes factuality. We further enable selective prediction at inference time (§3.4) based on the calibrated claim-level confidence. 

3.2 claim-aware reasoning protocol 

To operationalize claim-level modeling, we introduce a claim-aware reasoning protocol that structures generation and makes uncertainty explicit. As illustrated in Figure 1, given an input query x, the model first enters a <think> stage, where it constructs a reasoning trajectory, identifies candidate claims, and explicitly expresses uncertainty. The reasoning is 3Preprint. Under review. then transformed into a structured set of atomic claims via a <decompose> stage: {(ci, pi)}Ni=1,where each ci is independently verifiable and pi denotes its confidence. This representation grounds uncertainty in interpretable, claim-level units, enabling claim-level localization of uncertainty and providing a foundation for calibration. During training, all objectives operate on the structured outputs in the <think> and <decompose> stages, while at inference time, a final response is generated from the predicted claims via prompting (§3.4). By explicitly grounding uncertainty in atomic, verifiable claims, this protocol enables fine-grained calibration and precise attribution of errors. This structured representation forms the foundation for the multi-stage training pipeline. 

3.3 Multi-stage Training Pipeline 

A central design principle of our framework is to explicitly decouple calibration from factuality optimization. A naive approach might attempt to jointly optimize both objectives by combining them into a single RL reward. However, we find this joint optimization inherently creates conflicting learning dynamics, making calibration difficult to optimize effectively, as shown in §4.3. As the policy improves its factual correctness, the easiest way for the model to maximize a joint reward is to assign uniformly high confidence to all of its generated claims. This optimization shortcut leads to degenerate solutions characterized by severe overconfidence, destroying the model’s ability to estimate its uncertainty. To address these challenges, we propose a multi-stage training pipeline that structurally isolates these objectives: (1) Stage 1: Feasibility Induction (§3.3.1) establishes a constrained reasoning space where claims are well-formed and verifiable. (2) Stage 2: Calibration Optimization (§3.3.2) aligns confidence estimates with empirical correctness via preference learning. (3) Stage 3: Factuality Optimization (§3.3.3) maximizes factual accuracy using claim-level rewards. 

3.3.1 Stage1: Feasibility Induction 

Before optimizing for calibration or factuality, we first induce a feasible claim-centric reasoning space where the model follows the required format and generates relevant, verifiable claims. 

Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with confidence correction. We begin with SFT to teach the model the structured generation protocol. To construct the training data, we employ few-shot prompting with Llama3.1-8B-Instuct (Team, 2024), using manually crafted demon-strations 2 to guide the model in generating outputs in the <think> and <decompose> format, complete with explicit claim-level confidence reasoning. We discard rollouts that do not follow the required structure. To improve the quality of confidence labels, we further perform confidence correction on the retained valid rollouts. We evaluate the atomic claims extracted directly from the 

<decompose> block using an external verification pipeline, specifically VeriScore (Song et al., 2024), which verifies claims against retrieved evidence. Based on these correctness signals, we prompt an LLM 3 to simultaneously revise the confidence reasoning and scores within the <think> block, as well as the final confidence scores in the <decompose> block. Rather than using hard labels, we guide the model via prompting to assign high (>0.8), moderate (∼0.5), or low (near 0) confidence based on whether a claim is supported, inconclusive, or unsupported. Ultimately, this step initializes the model with the desired structured format while providing a calibrated prior over confidence estimation, which is critical for stabilizing subsequent training stages. 

RL for feasibility. Building on the SFT initialization, we further optimize the model using GRPO to enforce a set of feasibility constraints: (i) relevance , ensuring that the generated claims directly address the input query. 3.3.2 Stage 2: Calibration Optimization 

With a feasible reasoning space established, we next align the model’s confidence estimates with empirical correctness. Directly optimizing calibration via GRPO by incorporating proper scoring rules like the Brier score into the reward (Damani et al., 2025) provides weak and brittle learning signals that often fail to generalize. Calibration requires strict alignment between confidence and correctness at the claim level , whereas GRPO operates at the rollout level with varying claim content. Consequently, the reward differences between sampled rollouts are dominated by variations in claim content rather than calibration quality, leading to unstable training dynamics and noisy advantage estimation. To isolate confidence alignment from content variation, we cast calibration as a preference optimization problem.

With calibration aligned, we further optimize claim-level factuality using GRPO.

−1, otherwise, (1) where ϵ > 0 is a penalty term 7. A rollout satisfies the feasibility constraints if it strictly adheres to the required structural format, and its automated evaluation scores for relevance, verifiability, and faithfulness all exceed pre-defined thresholds. If any of these conditions are violated, the entire rollout is penalized with a reward of −1. The role of ϵ is to prevent the model from generating an excessive number of low-quality claims. Without this penalty, the model is incentivized to increase reward by producing a large number of claims, including many incorrect or low-confidence ones. By subtracting a constant cost per claim, the reward implicitly balances correctness and coverage, encouraging the model to generate claims that are both accurate and necessary. 

Optimization with masked rewards. As shown in Figure 2, we optimize this objective using GRPO, where rewards are applied exclusively to the tokens corresponding to the semantic content of the claims. Crucially, the tokens representing confidence reasoning and numerical confidence values are masked during advantage assignment. This ensures that factuality optimization strictly improves claim correctness without interfering with the calibrated confidence estimates learned in the previous preference optimization stage.

Importantly, the confidence threshold τ serves a different role from the training-time penalty ϵ: while ϵ regularizes claim generation during training, τ directly controls which claims are retained in the final output at inference time. At inference time, the final coherent answer is generated via prompting 8 conditioned on filtered claims. ## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup 

Following the data construction pipeline of Chen et al. (2025), we use their SFT and their RL prompts for all subsequent training stages, and adopt Llama3.1-8B-Instruct (Team, 2024) as the base model. Evaluation is conducted on four long-form factuality benchmarks: FactBench (Bayat et al., 2025), LongFact (Wei et al., 2024), Biography (Min et al., 2023), and FactRBench (Liu et al., 2025). we adopt VeriScore (Song et al., 2024) for factual accuracy ( Acc )evaluation. Unlike baselines that rely on VeriScore for claim extraction, our model explicitly outputs atomic claims, enabling us to skip the decomposition step and proceed directly to claim verification. To assess calibration, we report Expected Calibration Error ( ECE ), Brier score ( Brier ), and Area Under the ROC Curve ( AUROC ). Although ECE and Brier are standard calibration metrics, they can artificially reward models that conservatively predict the average dataset accuracy without actually resolving true from false claims. In contrast, AUROC directly measures the model’s ability to rank and separate correct claims from incorrect ones, making it a more robust indicator of discriminative calibration. We therefore place greater emphasis on AUROC in our evaluation. Further details of training data, benchmarks are provided in Appendix A.1 

Baselines. We compare CURE with the following baselines for long-form factual gen-eration and confidence estimation. Base LLM : The original model without post-training. We estimate claim-level confidence following LitCab (Liu et al., 2024) by extracting atomic claims, aligning them to generation spans, and aggregating token probabilities. L2RF : We compare against the Learning to Reason for Factuality framework (Chen et al., 2025), which designs a GRPO reward combining factual precision, response detail, and answer relevance to improve factuality. LitCab : A lightweight calibration method that learns a linear transfor-mation over final hidden states to adjust generation logits. We further include intermediate variants of our multi-stage pipeline (after SFT, feasibility optimization, calibration optimiza-tion, and factuality optimization) to isolate the contribution of each stage. 4.2 Main Results 

Table 1 reports the main results on three long-form factuality benchmarks using Llama3.1-8B-Instuct as the base model. Additional results on Qwen3-4B are presented in §4.5. We can draw the following key conclusions: 

CURE achieves superior factual accuracy. CURE achieves the highest claim-level accu-racy across all benchmarks, reaching 84.4 on FactBench, 90.2 on LongFact, and 65.9 on Biography. Compared to the strong RL baseline L2RF, this corresponds to consistent gains across datasets. Beyond the final performance, Table 1 reveals a clear stage-wise improve-ment pattern. The largest gains arise after introducing calibration optimization, which improves accuracy from 78.1 to 82.5 on FactBench, suggesting that aligning confidence with correctness also benefits factual generation. Subsequent factuality optimization further improves accuracy (82.5 to 84.4), indicating that calibration provides a strong foundation for downstream RL optimization. These results suggest that explicitly modeling uncertainty is not only beneficial for calibration, but also plays a key role in improving factual accuracy. 

CURE produces high-quality confidence estimates. CURE achieves the best calibration performance on FactBench and LongFact, and remains competitive on Biography particu-larly using the more informative metric AUROC. We note that the base LLM and LitCab achieve better calibration metrics on Biography primarily due to their lower accuracy, where the model tends to assign uniformly low confidence, leading to improved calibration scores. In contrast, CURE consistently produces high-quality confidence estimates across all three benchmarks. This demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of our approach for confidence estimation. 

Stage-wise improvements highlight the importance of decoupling. The staged design of CURE enables distinct improvements at different phases. Calibration optimization primarily improves confidence alignment, leading to substantial gains in AUROC (e.g., from 0.563 to 0.648 on FactBench), while also contributing to accuracy improvements. Factuality optimization further improves accuracy without degrading calibration, indicating that the two objectives are effectively disentangled. This behavior contrasts with joint optimization, where calibration performance remains limited despite improvements in accuracy (Table 2). Together, these results highlight the importance of decoupling calibration from factuality optimization. We provide additional ablation studies in §4.3 to further demonstrate why joint optimization leads to overconfident predictions and unstable calibration, and how our decoupled design resolves these issues. 

4.3 Analysis of Design Choices 

We conduct further analyses to validate the key design principles of our framework, focusing on understanding why joint optimization leads to overconfident predictions and how our decoupled design resolves this issue. Specifically, we examine the role of feasibility induction in establishing a reliable reasoning space, analyze the failure modes of joint calibration– factuality optimization, and investigate the effectiveness of DPO for calibration. 

The Effect of Feasibility RL in Stage 1 Training. While SFT initializes the reasoning format, it often fails to follow strict format constraints, leading to off-topic, unverifiable, or unfaithful claims. These issues undermine the subsequent training stages: unverifiable claims introduce substantial noise into the factuality reward, while unfaithful reasoning breaks the alignment between intermediate confidence and final claims, making reliable calibration infeasible. RL for feasibility mitigates these issues by enforcing format constraints. ## 5 Conclusion 

We propose CURE, a framework for improving long-form generattion factuality via claim-level calibration. Our key idea is that reducing hallucination in long-form generation requires not only optimizing correctness, but also teaching the model to estimate its un-certainty over individual claims. We realize this idea via the design of the claim-Aware reasoning protocol and a multi-stage training pipeline that first aligns confidence with empirical correctness and then improves factuality with token-masked RL. Across multi-ple long-form factuality benchmarks, CURE consistently improves factual accuracy and calibration quality over strong baselines, while preserving factual recall. Moreover, the resulting calibrated claim-level confidence estimates enable selective prediction, making model outputs more transparent, controllable, and reliable. Overall, our results show that fine-grained uncertainty modeling is a practical and effective path toward more trustworthy long-form generation.
